\section{Four basis states for two qubits}
\label{sec:ch08-four-basis-states}

A single qubit has two standard basis states, $\ketzero$ and $\ketone$. Two qubits have four combinations:
\begin{equation}
\ket{00},\qquad \ket{01},\qquad \ket{10},\qquad \ket{11}.
\label{eq:ch08-four-basis-states}
\end{equation}

In the label $\ket{01}$, the first digit belongs to the first qubit and the second digit belongs to the second qubit. \Cref{fig:ch08-four-basis-states} organizes the four labels as a two-by-two grid.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.91\textwidth]{fig_ch08_four_basis_states.pdf}
\caption{The four standard basis states of two qubits. The first digit records the first qubit's measurement result, and the second digit records the second qubit's result.}
\label{fig:ch08-four-basis-states}
\end{figure}

A measurement of the pair produces one of these four two-bit strings. For example, 10 means that the first qubit was measured as 1 and the second as 0.

\begin{misconception}
\textbf{Incorrect statement:} ``The four labels 00, 01, 10, and 11 mean that the system has four qubits.''

\medskip
\textbf{Correction:} The system has two qubits. Four is the number of possible standard-basis outcomes for the pair.
\end{misconception}

\section{Independent qubits and the product rule}
\label{sec:ch08-independent-probabilities}

Let
\begin{equation}
p=P(\text{first qubit}=1),\qquad
q=P(\text{second qubit}=1).
\label{eq:ch08-p-q-definitions}
\end{equation}
Then the probabilities of 0 are $1-p$ and $1-q$.

If the two qubits are prepared independently, the probability of a joint outcome is found by multiplying the probability for the first digit by the probability for the second digit. \Cref{fig:ch08-probability-tree} shows this as a branching process.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{fig_ch08_independent_probability_tree.pdf}
\caption{A probability tree for two independently prepared qubits. Each final branch corresponds to one joint outcome, and its probability is the product of the two branch probabilities.}
\label{fig:ch08-probability-tree}
\end{figure}

The four probabilities are summarized in \Cref{tab:ch08-independent-joint-probabilities}.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Outcome & Meaning & Probability for independent qubits \\
\midrule
00 & first is 0, second is 0 & $(1-p)(1-q)$ \\
01 & first is 0, second is 1 & $(1-p)q$ \\
10 & first is 1, second is 0 & $p(1-q)$ \\
11 & first is 1, second is 1 & $pq$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{The joint probabilities for two independently prepared qubits.}
\label{tab:ch08-independent-joint-probabilities}
\end{table}

For the app settings $p=1/2$ and $q=1/4$,
\begin{align}
P(00)&=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{3}{4}=\frac{3}{8}, &
P(01)&=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{4}=\frac{1}{8},\\
P(10)&=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{3}{4}=\frac{3}{8}, &
P(11)&=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{4}=\frac{1}{8}.
\end{align}
The four values add to 1, as every run must end in exactly one of the four outcomes.

\begin{coreidea}
For independent qubits, joint probabilities factor into one probability from the first qubit and one from the second.
\end{coreidea}

\section{A joint distribution describes the pair}
\label{sec:ch08-joint-distribution}

The list
\begin{equation}
\bigl(P(00),P(01),P(10),P(11)\bigr)
\end{equation}
is called a \emph{joint distribution}. It describes the two results together rather than describing either qubit alone.

We can recover the probability for one qubit by adding the joint outcomes that contain the result we want. For the first qubit,
\begin{align}
P(\text{first}=0)&=P(00)+P(01),\\
P(\text{first}=1)&=P(10)+P(11).
\end{align}
For the second qubit,
\begin{align}
P(\text{second}=0)&=P(00)+P(10),\\
P(\text{second}=1)&=P(01)+P(11).
\end{align}

For the independent formulas in \Cref{tab:ch08-independent-joint-probabilities}, these sums return the original values $p$ and $q$. For example,
\begin{equation}
P(\text{first}=1)=p(1-q)+pq=p.
\end{equation}

This distinction between the probability of each part and the joint probability of the pair will become essential in Chapters 9 and 10. Two systems can have similar individual probabilities but very different relationships between their paired outcomes.

\section{From four probabilities to four amplitudes}
\label{sec:ch08-two-qubit-state}

Probabilities are not the complete quantum state. Chapter 6 showed that amplitude signs and phases can affect later interference. A general pure state of two qubits is therefore written with four amplitudes:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi}
=\alpha_{00}\ket{00}
+\alpha_{01}\ket{01}
+\alpha_{10}\ket{10}
+\alpha_{11}\ket{11}.
\label{eq:ch08-general-two-qubit-state}
\end{equation}
The corresponding standard-basis measurement probabilities are
\begin{equation}
P(ij)=|\alpha_{ij}|^2,
\qquad ij\in\{00,01,10,11\},
\label{eq:ch08-two-qubit-born-rule}
\end{equation}
and normalization requires
\begin{equation}
|\alpha_{00}|^2+|\alpha_{01}|^2+|\alpha_{10}|^2+|\alpha_{11}|^2=1.
\end{equation}

This notation does not mean that one measurement reveals four values. It means that four possible basis outcomes have amplitudes before measurement. One standard-basis measurement still produces one two-bit result. This is the usual two-qubit state description in quantum information \citep{nielsenChuang2010,mermin2007quantumComputerScience}.

\begin{goingdeeper}
If two pure qubit states are prepared separately,
\begin{equation}
\ket{a}=a_0\ketzero+a_1\ketone,
\qquad
\ket{b}=b_0\ketzero+b_1\ketone,
\end{equation}
then their joint state is the tensor product
\begin{align}
\ket{a}\otimes\ket{b}
={}&a_0b_0\ket{00}+a_0b_1\ket{01}\\
&+a_1b_0\ket{10}+a_1b_1\ket{11}.
\label{eq:ch08-product-amplitudes}
\end{align}
The four amplitudes factor into one amplitude from each qubit. Not every two-qubit state can be written this way. Chapter 9 studies what happens when the two-qubit state cannot be separated into two individual state descriptions.
\end{goingdeeper}

\section{Why the state space doubles}
\label{sec:ch08-state-space-growth}

Each qubit contributes two possible digits, 0 and 1. Adding one qubit therefore doubles the number of basis labels:
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccl}
\toprule
Number of qubits & Number of basis states & Example labels \\
\midrule
1 & $2$ & 0, 1 \\
2 & $4$ & 00, 01, 10, 11 \\
3 & $8$ & 000 through 111 \\
4 & $16$ & 0000 through 1111 \\
$n$ & $2^n$ & all binary strings of length $n$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Every added qubit doubles the number of standard basis states.}
\label{tab:ch08-state-space-growth}
\end{table}

For $n$ qubits, a standard basis label is an $n$-bit string. There are $2^n$ such strings. This rapid growth is one reason that describing large quantum systems becomes difficult on a classical computer \citep{preskill1998lectureNotes,nielsenChuang2010}.

But the size of the state description must not be confused with the amount of information obtained from one measurement.

\begin{misconception}
\textbf{Incorrect statement:} ``Because an $n$-qubit state has $2^n$ amplitudes, one measurement reads all $2^n$ values.''

\medskip
\textbf{Correction:} One standard-basis measurement returns one $n$-bit string. Quantum algorithms must use gates and interference to arrange the amplitudes so that a useful property becomes likely to appear in that limited output.
\end{misconception}

\section{Reading a two-qubit circuit}
\label{sec:ch08-circuit-reading}

A quantum circuit diagram is a timeline. Each horizontal line tracks one qubit. The left side is earlier, and the right side is later. A box on one line is a gate applied to that qubit at that point.

\Cref{fig:ch08-circuit-reading} shows three important visual features. The $H$ and $X$ boxes are local gates because each acts on one wire. The connected control and target symbols represent one operation acting on the two-qubit system together. Measurement boxes appear near the end because they convert the quantum state into visible classical results.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{fig_ch08_two_line_circuit_reading.pdf}
\caption{How to read a two-qubit circuit. Follow each wire from left to right. A box on one wire is a local gate, while a vertical connection indicates an operation involving both qubits.}
\label{fig:ch08-circuit-reading}
\end{figure}

When two gate symbols are placed at the same horizontal position but are not connected, they represent separate operations at the same circuit stage. A visible connection between the wires has a different meaning: the operation depends on the two-qubit system as a whole.

\section{What changes when the wires are connected?}
\label{sec:ch08-connected-preview}

Two independently prepared qubits begin with separate probability or amplitude descriptions. A connecting gate can change the joint state so that the relationship between the outcomes becomes important.

A connecting gate does not automatically create entanglement for every input. For example, some definite input states remain definite product states after a controlled operation. Other inputs can produce a state that no longer separates into one state for the first qubit and one state for the second.

Chapter 9 compares three cases carefully:
\begin{itemize}
  \item independently prepared qubits;
  \item a classically correlated mixture;
  \item an entangled Bell state.
\end{itemize}
The four measurement labels remain 00, 01, 10, and 11. What changes is the relationship among their amplitudes and probabilities.

\begin{coreidea}
Adding a second qubit creates four basis outcomes. Connecting the two qubits can make the relationship among those outcomes part of the state itself.
\end{coreidea}

\section{Chapter summary}
\label{sec:ch08-summary}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Two qubits have four standard basis states: $\ket{00}$, $\ket{01}$, $\ket{10}$, and $\ket{11}$.
  \item One measurement of two qubits produces one two-bit result.
  \item For independently prepared qubits, joint probabilities are products of individual probabilities.
  \item A joint distribution describes the pair and can contain more information than either individual distribution alone.
  \item A general two-qubit pure state has four amplitudes whose squared magnitudes give the four standard-basis probabilities.
  \item Each added qubit doubles the number of basis states, giving $2^n$ basis labels for $n$ qubits.
  \item A two-line circuit is read from left to right; a connection between the lines indicates a joint operation.
\end{itemize}
