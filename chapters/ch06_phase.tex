\chapter[Phase and Interference]{Phase: Information Hidden from a Direct Measurement}
\label{chap:phase}

\begin{learningobjectives}
By the end of this chapter, the reader should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item distinguish a measurement probability from a probability amplitude;
  \item explain why two states can give the same direct measurement statistics but behave differently later;
  \item use the states $\ketplus$ and $\ketminus$ as a first example of relative phase;
  \item interpret the circuits $H H$ and $H Z H$ as interference experiments;
  \item explain constructive and destructive interference without saying that a quantum computer simply ``tries all answers'';
  \item describe, at an introductory level, why phase is useful in quantum algorithms.
\end{itemize}
\end{learningobjectives}

\begin{openingquestion}
Can two quantum states give exactly the same measurement probabilities and still be different states?
\end{openingquestion}

\section{Try it first: two circuits that look almost the same}
\label{sec:ch06-try-first}

\begin{tryit}
Open \applevel{4} in the companion app. Before reading the explanation, run two short circuits and write down your prediction.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Circuit A: start with $\ketzero$, apply $H$, apply $H$ again, and measure.
  \item Circuit B: start with $\ketzero$, apply $H$, apply $Z$, apply $H$, and measure.
\end{enumerate}

For each circuit, predict whether the ideal simulator will return 0 every time, 1 every time, or 0 and 1 with equal probability. Then run the circuits and compare your prediction with the result.
\end{tryit}

The two circuits differ by only one middle gate, the $Z$ gate, yet their final outcomes differ. The question is what the $Z$ gate changed. That change is not visible if we measure immediately after the middle step.

\section{What the direct measurement misses}
\label{sec:ch06-direct-measurement-misses}

Start with the state $\ketzero$. Applying a Hadamard gate produces
\begin{equation}
\ketplus=\frac{\ketzero+\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}.
\label{eq:ket-plus}
\end{equation}
If we measure this state in the usual 0/1 basis, we get 0 with probability $1/2$ and 1 with probability $1/2$.

Now apply a $Z$ gate after the first $H$ gate. The $Z$ gate changes the sign of the $\ketone$ part:
\begin{equation}
Z\ketplus=\frac{\ketzero-\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}=\ketminus.
\label{eq:z-creates-ket-minus}
\end{equation}
If we measure $\ketminus$ directly in the usual 0/1 basis, we again get 0 with probability $1/2$ and 1 with probability $1/2$.

This is the key surprise. The direct probabilities are the same, but the states are not the same. \Cref{fig:ch06-hidden-phase-concept} shows the difference. The probability view looks identical. The amplitude view shows the hidden relative sign.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{fig_ch06_hidden_phase_concept.pdf}
\caption{The states $\ketplus$ and $\ketminus$ give the same direct 0/1 probabilities, but their amplitudes differ by a relative sign. A direct measurement in the 0/1 basis misses this sign difference. A later gate can make it visible.}
\label{fig:ch06-hidden-phase-concept}
\end{figure}

\begin{coreidea}
Quantum probabilities are not the whole state. A later gate can reveal information that was hidden from a direct measurement.
\end{coreidea}

\section{Amplitudes before probabilities}
\label{sec:ch06-amplitudes-before-probability}

In a one-qubit state
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi}=\alpha\ketzero+\beta\ketone,
\label{eq:one-qubit-state}
\end{equation}
the numbers $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are called amplitudes. To get measurement probabilities in the 0/1 basis, we square their magnitudes:
\begin{equation}
P(0)=|\alpha|^2,\qquad P(1)=|\beta|^2.
\label{eq:born-rule-one-qubit}
\end{equation}
This probability rule is one of the basic rules of quantum theory \citep{nielsenChuang2010,mermin2007quantumComputerScience}.

For the sign-based examples in this chapter, the amplitudes are real numbers. This means that the sign disappears when we square:
\begin{equation}
\left(+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2=\frac12,
\qquad
\left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2=\frac12.
\label{eq:sign-squares}
\end{equation}

\Cref{tab:ch06-amplitude-probability} compares the amplitudes and probabilities directly.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
State & Amplitude for 0 & Amplitude for 1 & $P(0)$ & $P(1)$ \\
\midrule
$\ketplus$ & $+1/\sqrt{2}$ & $+1/\sqrt{2}$ & $1/2$ & $1/2$ \\
$\ketminus$ & $+1/\sqrt{2}$ & $-1/\sqrt{2}$ & $1/2$ & $1/2$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{The direct probabilities are the same, but the relative signs of the amplitudes are different.}
\label{tab:ch06-amplitude-probability}
\end{table}

The plus sign and the minus sign become the same probability if we measure immediately in the 0/1 basis. But the signs have not disappeared from the state. They can matter when amplitudes are combined again.

\begin{misconception}
\textbf{Incorrect statement:} ``If two states give the same probabilities, they are the same state.''

\medskip
\textbf{Correction:} The statement is incomplete. Two states can give the same probabilities in one measurement basis and still differ in their relative phase. The circuits $H H$ and $H Z H$ are a direct counterexample.
\end{misconception}

\section{Relative sign as the first form of phase}
\label{sec:ch06-relative-sign}

In this first example, phase appears as a sign.

The state $\ketplus$ has a plus sign between the two parts:
\begin{equation}
\ketplus=\frac{\ketzero+\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{equation}
The state $\ketminus$ has a minus sign between the two parts:
\begin{equation}
\ketminus=\frac{\ketzero-\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{equation}

The important word is \emph{between}. The difference is not attached to the whole state. It is a relative difference between the $\ketzero$ part and the $\ketone$ part.

A relative sign is the simplest case of a relative phase. More generally, relative phase is described by an angle: the plus and minus cases correspond to phase differences of $0$ and $\pi$. For now, it is enough to remember this:

\begin{coreidea}
A relative phase is information about how parts of a quantum state are arranged before they are recombined.
\end{coreidea}

\section{Why the final Hadamard reveals the difference}
\label{sec:ch06-final-hadamard}

The last $H$ gate acts like a recombination step. It takes the two parts of the state and combines them into new output amplitudes.

The Hadamard gate acts as follows:
\begin{align}
H\ketzero &= \frac{\ketzero+\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}, \\
H\ketone &= \frac{\ketzero-\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{align}
It also works in the reverse direction:
\begin{align}
H\ketplus &= \ketzero, \\
H\ketminus &= \ketone.
\end{align}

So if the middle state is $\ketplus$, the final $H$ sends it to $\ketzero$. If the middle state is $\ketminus$, the final $H$ sends it to $\ketone$.

\Cref{fig:ch06-recombination-flow} explains the process as a flow. The final $H$ does not merely ``look at'' the middle state. It changes the basis in which the sign difference is visible.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{fig_ch06_recombination_flow.pdf}
\caption{The final Hadamard gate recombines amplitudes. For $\ketplus$, output 0 survives and output 1 cancels. For $\ketminus$, output 0 cancels and output 1 survives. This is the interference step that makes the hidden relative sign visible.}
\label{fig:ch06-recombination-flow}
\end{figure}

The same idea is summarized symbolically in \Cref{tab:ch06-recombination-summary}.

Circuit & Middle state & Recombination by final $H$ & Final result \\
\midrule

\bottomrule

\section{Constructive and destructive interference}
\label{sec:ch06-constructive-destructive}

The words \emph{constructive interference} and \emph{destructive interference} describe how amplitudes combine.

Constructive interference means that amplitudes add in a way that makes an outcome more likely.

Destructive interference means that amplitudes cancel in a way that makes an outcome less likely or impossible.

In the $H H$ circuit, the amplitudes recombine so that the 0 outcome survives and the 1 outcome cancels. In the $H Z H$ circuit, the relative sign has changed. Now the 0 outcome cancels and the 1 outcome survives.

This is the key point: the final measurement does not simply reveal a hidden classical bit. It reveals the result of a controlled recombination of amplitudes.

\section{A phase sweep: from bright to dark}
\label{sec:ch06-phase-sweep}

\applevel{11} generalizes the sign example. Instead of only using a plus sign or a minus sign, the app lets the learner choose a phase difference. It labels the two output ports as bright and dark. These are interferometer-style names; the qubit does not literally become brighter or darker.

For an ideal balanced two-path interference experiment, the visual pattern is summarized by
\begin{equation}
P(\mathrm{bright})=\frac{1+\cos(\Delta\phi)}{2},
\qquad
P(\mathrm{dark})=1-P(\mathrm{bright}).
\label{eq:bright-dark-probability}
\end{equation}
Here $\Delta\phi$ is the phase difference between two paths or two parts of the state. The visual experiment shows the pattern first; the formula then summarizes it.

\Cref{fig:ch06-phase-sweep} shows the pattern. At $0^\circ$, the bright output has probability 1. At $180^\circ$, the bright output has probability 0 and the dark output has probability 1. At $90^\circ$ and $270^\circ$, the two outputs are equally likely.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{fig_ch06_phase_sweep.pdf}
\caption{Changing the relative phase moves probability between the bright and dark outputs. The cases $0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$ show complete constructive and destructive interference in opposite outputs. The cases $90^\circ$ and $270^\circ$ give equal probabilities in this simple model.}
\label{fig:ch06-phase-sweep}
\end{figure}

This is why phase is often easier to understand through an interactive experiment than through a formula alone. The learner can move from fully bright to fully dark by changing the phase.

\begin{misconception}
\textbf{Incorrect statement:} ``The dark output means that nothing happened there.''

\medskip
\textbf{Correction:} The dark output is a result of cancellation. It is not a place where the experiment was ignored. It is an output whose amplitude has been reduced by destructive interference.
\end{misconception}

\textbf{Quick check 6.4}

  \item What is $P(\mathrm{bright})$ at $180^\circ$?
  \item What does destructive interference reduce: an amplitude or an already observed measurement result?
\end{enumerate}
\end{checkpoint}

\section{What the wave analogy helps with}
\label{sec:ch06-wave-analogy}

It is helpful to compare this with waves on water, sound waves, or light waves. When two waves meet crest-to-crest, they can reinforce each other. When a crest meets a trough, they can cancel. Interferometer examples are often used as an intuitive bridge into quantum interference \citep{preskill1998lectureNotes}.

\Cref{fig:ch06-wave-analogy-limits} shows how the analogy should be used. The helpful part is the idea of addition and cancellation. The limit is that a quantum measurement does not show a continuous wave height everywhere; it gives one observed outcome in a single shot.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{fig_ch06_wave_analogy_limits.pdf}
\caption{The wave analogy is useful for understanding addition and cancellation, but it should not be taken literally. Quantum amplitudes are used to predict measurement probabilities, and a single measurement gives one observed outcome.}
\label{fig:ch06-wave-analogy-limits}
\end{figure}

The analogy is therefore limited. A quantum state is not a tiny water wave. The analogy concerns how amplitudes combine; a single measurement still gives one observed outcome.

\begin{coreidea}
The wave analogy helps explain addition and cancellation, but the quantum state is a probability-amplitude description, not an ordinary water wave.
\end{coreidea}

\section{Why phase matters for algorithms}
\label{sec:ch06-phase-algorithms}

Quantum algorithms do not work merely by creating many possibilities. Creating many possibilities is only the beginning. The useful part is arranging amplitudes so that unhelpful outcomes cancel and useful patterns remain.

Chapter 7 will generalize this phase-marking-and-recombination pattern. Chapter 11 will then present the Deutsch algorithm as the first complete algorithmic example. In that algorithm, an oracle places information about a function into a phase pattern, and a final Hadamard gate converts the pattern into a measurable 0 or 1 on the first qubit \citep{deutsch1985quantumTheory}. This is the same lesson as $H H$ versus $H Z H$, but used for a computational question.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Step & Interference chapter & Deutsch chapter \\
\midrule
Prepare possibilities & create $\ketplus$ & prepare a superposition of inputs \\
Mark phase & apply $Z$ & apply the oracle $U_f$ \\
Recombine & apply final $H$ & apply final $H$ to the first qubit \\
Measure pattern & get 0 or 1 & classify constant or balanced \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{The Deutsch algorithm uses the same phase-and-recombination idea in a computational setting.}
\label{tab:ch06-deutsch-bridge}
\end{table}

\Cref{tab:ch06-deutsch-bridge} previews the structure of the Deutsch algorithm. This is a safer way to describe quantum algorithms than saying that the computer ``tries every answer and reads the right one.'' A quantum algorithm must make the amplitudes interfere in a useful way before measurement.

\begin{misconception}
\textbf{Incorrect statement:} ``A quantum computer gets its power because it tries all answers at once and then prints the correct answer.''

\medskip
\textbf{Correction:} A useful quantum algorithm must control phases and interference. Measurement gives one outcome, so the circuit must be designed so that useful outcomes become likely and unhelpful outcomes cancel.
\end{misconception}

\section{Global phase and relative phase}
\label{sec:ch06-global-relative-phase}

There is one more distinction that prevents many mistakes.

Multiplying the whole state by the same minus sign does not change any measurement prediction:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi}\quad\text{and}\quad -\ket{\psi}
\end{equation}
represent the same physical state. The minus sign is then a global phase.

By contrast, changing the sign of only one part changes a relative phase:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\ketzero+\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\frac{\ketzero-\ketone}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{equation}

The $H Z H$ experiment is sensitive to this relative difference. A relative phase can affect interference; a global phase cannot. The distinction between global and relative phase is standard in quantum mechanics and quantum information \citep{nielsenChuang2010,mermin2007quantumComputerScience}.

\begin{goingdeeper}
A general one-qubit state can be written as
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi}=\alpha\ketzero+\beta\ketone,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ may be complex numbers. After removing an irrelevant global phase, a normalized one-qubit pure state can also be written as
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi}
=
\cos\frac{\theta}{2}\ketzero
+
e^{i\phi}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\ketone.
\end{equation}
The angle $\phi$ is the relative phase. The sign examples in the main text correspond to special cases of this more general expression.
\end{goingdeeper}

\section{Chapter summary}
\label{sec:ch06-summary}

The main ideas of this chapter are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A quantum state contains amplitudes, not only probabilities.
  \item The sign or phase of an amplitude may be invisible in one direct measurement.
  \item A later gate can recombine amplitudes and reveal a relative phase.
  \item Constructive interference makes an outcome more likely.
  \item Destructive interference makes an outcome less likely or impossible.
  \item Quantum algorithms use phase and interference to make useful measurement outcomes more likely.
\end{itemize}

The Deutsch algorithm chapter will use these ideas in a complete circuit.
